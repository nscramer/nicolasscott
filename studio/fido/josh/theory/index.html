Proving Grandma’s Rule

In 1959, a young psychologist named David Premack proved in a laboratory what grandmothers have known for generations: that a more preferred behavior can reinforce (i.e. make happen more frequently) a preceding, less preferred behavior.

Translated: "If you eat your vegetables, you can have dessert."

The games we’ve been playing show our dogs that it’s worth doing something that they’re not wild about if it’s the key to unlocking something awesome.

This idea is called the Premack Principle.

With Juno, this means that whatever she does immediately before doing what she wants will be rewarded and repeated.

These training sessions have been about reinforcing a patient demeanor with food. They're a great place to start. But this needn't be the only way to use Premack.

What are Juno’s default behaviors?

Start thinking about what other kinds of default behaviors (what should your dog do when she doesn’t know what to do?) you'd like Juno to have. For Fitz and me, that means sitting for meals or when he'd like to lunge after a stick on the sidewalk.

Remember, just like with people, it’s more effective to show our dogs what they should do instead of only punishing what they shouldn’t.

These exercises are a great foundation for starting work on impulse control, but they're good for dogs of any skill level. Knowledge isn't enough. It's only by being consistent and putting it into practice each day that we stay sharp.
